How the Debt-to-Income Ratio Calculator Works

The debt-to-income (DTI) ratio is calculated using the formula:

This ratio is a key indicator of your financial health and is widely used by lenders to evaluate your ability to manage monthly payments and repay debts. It represents the percentage of your monthly gross income that goes toward paying debts.

Lenders typically prefer a DTI of 36% or less, with no more than 28% going toward housing expenses. A lower DTI ratio indicates a better balance between debt and income.

Frequently Asked Questions

What debts are included in the DTI calculation?
Common debts included in DTI calculations are: mortgage/rent, auto loans, student loans, credit card minimum payments, personal loans, and child support/alimony. Assets like savings accounts and investment portfolios are not considered.
How does DTI affect loan approval?
A lower DTI ratio improves your chances of loan approval and better interest rates. Most lenders prefer a DTI of 36% or less, though some accept up to 43% for qualified borrowers. A high DTI suggests you may be overextended financially.
How can I improve my DTI ratio?
You can improve your DTI by either reducing your debt (paying down balances, consolidating loans) or increasing your income (getting a raise, finding additional employment). Avoid taking on new debt until your DTI improves.
